# Product Studio

*Operating design for the Faculty's Validate-stage program. Companion to the Engineering Entrepreneurship Programming Revamp, which covers the strategy, the consolidated budget, and the pipeline-level targets.*

## Overview

Product Studio is the Faculty of Engineering's Validate-stage program: a year-round program that teaches engineering and computer science students how to take an idea through the full product lifecycle, from finding a real problem to launching a working product. Its single purpose is to move students to the point where they meet MakerLaunch's entry threshold, which is evidence of customer discovery or a working prototype.

Product Studio is the program the programming plan refers to as the new Validate-stage build. This document is its operating design: curriculum, formats, delivery model, readiness gate, and program metrics. For the strategic rationale, the four-stage pipeline, the consolidated budget, and the pipeline-level targets, see the plan.

It is delivered through three connected formats across the year: an open Foundations workshop series, short intensive Build Sprints, and a milestone-driven Studio Cohort. A student can start with one drop-in workshop and progress, at their own pace, to a MakerLaunch-ready venture.

*Note on the name. Product Studio is a working title pending confirmation by the Program Director. If the program is renamed, the change applies throughout.*

| At a glance | |
| --- | --- |
| Audience | Engineering and computer science students, undergraduate and graduate, treated as one group |
| Pipeline stages | Explore and Validate (MakerLaunch operates at Build) |
| Core outcome | Students who meet the MakerLaunch readiness gate, with documented evidence |

## How Product Studio fits

Product Studio operates across the Explore and Validate stages of the Faculty pipeline. MakerLaunch operates at Build and receives Product Studio graduates. The Foundations Series sits at the top of the funnel in Explore, open to any student; the Studio Cohort does the committed Validate work that produces MakerLaunch-ready ventures.

The program complements existing Explore touchpoints, such as the GNG courses, capstone projects, case competitions with Telfer, the speaker series, and the Engineering Pitch Competition, rather than replacing them. It coordinates with the campus-wide Entrepreneurship Hub rather than duplicating it: students who want broader, cross-faculty programming are referred to eHub, and the Faculty can position itself as advisor to engineering-affiliated companies in eHub programs.

**Relationship to GNG courses.** GNG 1103 and GNG 2101 teach engineering design and a first taste of building for a user. Product Studio picks up where they leave off and adds the commercial dimension: not only whether you can build it, but whether you should, for whom, and how it reaches them. The GNG 4120 entrepreneurial stream described in the plan is the formal off-ramp from the classroom into Product Studio's Validate work.

## Target audience

Product Studio serves undergraduate and graduate students in the Faculty of Engineering, including computer science students in the School of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science. Engineering and computer science students are treated as one audience, with build-stage content tracked so hardware-oriented and software-oriented students each get relevant instruction. No prior business background is assumed; the program is built for technical students who can build things but have not yet learned how to decide what to build or how to bring it to people.

| Segment | Who they are | Which format serves them |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Curious | Interested, but with no idea or an untested one. They need exposure and basic skills. | Foundations Series and Build Sprints |
| The Committed | Have an idea and will work on it outside class. They need structure, mentorship, and milestones. | Studio Cohort |
| The Ready | Have met the readiness gate and should move on. | Handoff to MakerLaunch |

## Curriculum and competencies

Product Studio is organized around the six phases of the product lifecycle. Each phase builds one product competency and produces specific evidence. The same curriculum is taught in sequence in the Studio Cohort, offered phase by phase in the Foundations Series, and compressed into single-phase Build Sprints. Using one curriculum across all three formats is what keeps the program deliverable by one staff member.

| Phase | Competency: what the student can do | Output and evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Discover | Identify and validate a real problem through customer and user research | A validated problem statement, supported by evidence from at least twenty conversations |
| 2. Define | Translate findings into a value proposition and a scoped minimum viable product | A value proposition and an MVP definition |
| 3. Design | Produce a testable design or prototype that addresses the problem | Wireframes or mockups, a prototype |
| 4. Build | Create a working MVP or functional prototype | A working MVP (software) or functional prototype (hardware) |
| 5. Validate | Test with real users and decide whether to continue, change, or stop | Usability or pilot results, an iteration log, a persevere-or-pivot decision |
| 6. Launch | Plan how the product reaches its first users and gather early traction | A launch plan, first traction signals |

The Discover and Build outputs map directly to the MakerLaunch readiness gate: a student who demonstrates either one meets the entry threshold. Reaching the gate is a by-product of the curriculum, not a separate task.

The Design phase is common to hardware and software students, since fast, low-cost prototyping applies to both. The Build phase splits into parallel software and hardware tracks. The specific build tools change quickly and are reviewed each year (Appendix C). Sample sessions for every phase are in Appendix A.

## Program architecture: three formats

Product Studio runs three formats that share one curriculum. A student can enter at any one and move between them.

| Format | What it is | Commitment and audience |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Foundations Series | Open, drop-in-friendly workshops across fall and winter, each covering one piece of the lifecycle | Low. No application. Widest audience, top of the funnel. |
| Build Sprints | Short intensive formats that compress one phase into a concentrated burst, for example a weekend | Medium. Creates momentum and deadlines. Well suited to partner or alumni delivery. |
| Studio Cohort | A term-long applied cohort taking an idea through the full lifecycle against milestones | High. Committed teams, capped to staff capacity. Direct feeder into MakerLaunch. |

**Foundations Series.** Open workshops through the fall and winter terms, each covering one part of the lifecycle. No application and no obligation to attend the whole series. Foundations builds baseline skills, reaches the widest audience, and is the top of the funnel.

**Build Sprints.** Short, intensive, hands-on formats that compress one phase into a concentrated burst, such as a weekend or a reading-week intensive. Examples include a Discovery Sprint, a Prototyping Sprint, an MVP Build Weekend, and a Launch Sprint. Sprints create momentum, give students a deadline, and turn casual interest into real progress. They suit partner or alumni delivery.

**Studio Cohort.** The capstone format and the program's core. A term-long applied cohort in which committed teams take an idea through the full lifecycle against structured milestones, ending at the MakerLaunch readiness gate. This is where staff mentorship concentrates and where the program's main outcome is produced. Cohort size is capped to match staffing. It is the funded cohort referred to in the plan and the direct feeder into MakerLaunch.

**How they fit together.** A typical path runs from a Foundations workshop, to a Build Sprint that produces first momentum, to the Studio Cohort that drives a venture to readiness, and then to MakerLaunch. Students need not follow the full path. Some take a single workshop and stop; some enter directly at the Studio Cohort with an idea in hand. The structure is a set of on-ramps, not a fixed track.

## Delivery calendar

Product Studio runs across the full year. The grid below shows a representative year at steady state; year one is lighter, as set out in the roadmap. This is the program's own cadence and nests inside the master pipeline calendar in the plan.

| Term | Foundations Series | Build Sprints | Studio Cohort |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fall (Sep to Dec) | Weekly or biweekly, Discover through Design | One sprint, for example a Discovery Sprint | Applications open, cohort begins, first half of the lifecycle |
| Winter (Jan to Apr) | Continues, Build through Launch | One or two sprints, for example an MVP Build Weekend | Continues, reaches the readiness gate, qualifying teams referred to MakerLaunch |
| Spring and summer (May to Aug) | Light offering or none, by capacity | Optional summer intensive | Aligns with the Simon Nehme Summer Entrepreneurship School as a summer validation vehicle |

The summer alignment connects Product Studio to existing validation infrastructure and to the CEWIL iHUB funding work underway. The MakerLaunch summer cohort continues at the Build stage and receives teams that reach readiness.

## Pedagogy and delivery model

Product Studio is hands-on and project-based. Students learn by doing the work on a real or chosen idea, not by sitting through lectures. Every workshop ends with an action to take away, every sprint produces an artifact, and the cohort is organized entirely around producing evidence.

Delivery is designed for a single staff member. The Program Manager curates and coordinates the program rather than personally delivering every session. Three mechanisms make this work:

- **Partner and guest delivery.** Foundations workshops and Build Sprints suit delivery by alumni founders, industry practitioners, Telfer faculty, and eHub partners. The Program Manager designs the curriculum and recruits facilitators.
- **Reusable materials.** Each workshop has a repeatable format and a reusable resource pack, so a session can run again with little preparation and can be handed to a guest facilitator.
- **Student facilitators and mentors.** Experienced students and MakerLaunch alumni support sessions and mentor cohort teams, extending the program's reach and feeding the alumni flywheel.

Staff time concentrates on the Studio Cohort, where mentorship and milestone reviews need the Program Manager's direct involvement. Foundations and Build Sprints run largely on curated and partner-delivered content.

## Readiness gate and MakerLaunch handoff

The point of Product Studio is to produce MakerLaunch-ready ventures. MakerLaunch's entry threshold, adopted from Georgia Tech CREATE-X, is evidence of customer discovery to validate a market problem, or a working prototype. Product Studio is built so students reach this threshold as a natural result of the curriculum. There are two paths:

- **The discovery path.** A student who completes the Discover phase with rigour has interview evidence from at least twenty conversations and a validated problem statement, which meets the customer-discovery criterion.
- **The prototype path.** A student who completes the Design and Build phases has a working prototype addressing an articulated user problem, which meets the working-prototype criterion.

Most Studio Cohort teams meet both by the end of the term. Foundations and Build Sprint participants may meet one on a faster, lighter path.

**The handoff.** When a team meets the gate, the Program Manager refers them to MakerLaunch with a short readiness summary documenting the evidence. Because the same person leads both programs, the handoff is direct, and the readiness summary doubles as a stronger, evidence-based MakerLaunch application. This is the mechanism that fixes the original problem: applicants arrive with documented evidence of readiness, which is what advisors reported was missing.

## Facilitation and resourcing

**Staffing.** The program is led by the Program Manager, MakerLaunch, as part of the existing role. The model assumes no new full-time staff. Capacity is extended through guest facilitators, student facilitators, and mentors.

**Facilitators and mentors.** Sources include MakerLaunch alumni, Ottawa-area founders and practitioners, Telfer faculty for commercial topics, EECS faculty for software build topics, and eHub partners. A facilitator pool built from alumni strengthens the alumni flywheel the revamp depends on.

**Space.** The program uses the existing CEED space allocation, including existing rooms and the makerspace. Build Sprints can use the same space in a concentrated block. The plan proposes piloting a higher-traffic working space to raise visibility.

**Tools and software.** The program needs design and prototyping software, for example Figma under education licensing, and access to current no-code and AI-assisted build tools. A short, current tool list is kept in Appendix C and reviewed each year.

**Budget.** Product Studio does not carry a separate budget. Its costs sit within the Year 1 operating budget in the plan, chiefly validation micro-funding of $500 to $2,500 per team, facilitator and guest honoraria, prototyping materials, and design and prototyping software. See the plan's resources section for the consolidated figure.

## Metrics

Product Studio is measured on whether it produces ready MakerLaunch applicants, not on attendance alone. These program metrics feed the pipeline-level outcomes in the plan; they are not a separate scorecard. The single most important measure is advisor-rated quality of the MakerLaunch applicants Product Studio produces, since that was the original problem.

| Category | Metric | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reach | Unique students reached, engineering and computer science split, workshop attendance | Shows the top of the funnel is filling across both disciplines |
| Engagement | Foundations completion, sprint participation, cohort retention | Shows students are progressing, not just sampling |
| Readiness | Share of cohort teams that meet the MakerLaunch gate | The program's core outcome |
| Conversion | Number and share of participants who apply to and enter MakerLaunch, with advisor-rated quality | The direct test of whether the pipeline problem is being solved; this is the plan's applications metric measured upstream |

Downstream venture formation, progression into Build, and Founder Net Promoter Score are tracked as pipeline outcomes in the plan rather than duplicated here.

## Risks and mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
| --- | --- |
| Low initial uptake | Integrate with GNG courses for a built-in audience, market through existing Faculty channels, and consider light credit or recognition for participation |
| Single-staff overextension | Phase the rollout, lean on partner and alumni delivery, cap cohort size, and concentrate staff time on the cohort |
| Imbalance between engineering and computer science engagement | Build a distinct software build track, partner with EECS faculty, and recruit computer science alumni as facilitators |
| Duplication with eHub programming | Position Product Studio as Faculty-specific, coordinate the calendar with eHub, and refer students across rather than competing |
| Facilitator pipeline runs dry | Treat the facilitator pool as a designed alumni flywheel, with named roles and recognition, rather than ad hoc recruiting |
| Weak link to MakerLaunch in practice | Make the readiness summary and direct referral a required step, owned by the same person who leads both programs |

## Implementation roadmap

The rollout starts light and nests inside the plan's three-year phasing. Year one runs the Foundations Series and one Studio Cohort, with a small number of sprints, matching the single-staff constraint.

| Phase and timing | Key actions | Milestone |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Phase 0, design and approval (Summer 2026) | Finalize the curriculum, confirm the name, recruit a first set of facilitators, set the budget with the Director, and seek approval | Dean approval to launch |
| Phase 1, year-one launch (Fall 2026) | Run the first Foundations Series (Discover through Design), one Discovery Sprint, and open the first Studio Cohort | First cohort underway |
| Phase 2, first full cycle (Winter 2027) | Complete the Foundations Series (Build through Launch), run an MVP Build Weekend, and bring the first cohort to the readiness gate | First teams reach the gate and enter MakerLaunch |
| Phase 3, summer alignment (Spring and summer 2027) | Align with the Simon Nehme Summer Entrepreneurship School, run an optional summer intensive, and review year-one metrics | Year-one review complete |
| Phase 4, scale-up (from Fall 2027) | Add a fuller set of Build Sprints, grow the cohort within capacity, and expand the facilitator pool | Program at steady state |

## Appendices

### Appendix A. Sample sessions by phase

These are illustrative, not fixed.

- **Discover.** Problem framing and spotting opportunities; customer and user discovery (Steve Blank's approach); interview technique and avoiding leading questions; Jobs to be Done; mapping the market and the competition.
- **Define.** Synthesizing research with affinity mapping and personas; journey mapping; value-proposition design; scoping the minimum viable product.
- **Design.** Design thinking and the double diamond; wireframing and information architecture; interaction and usability basics; rapid prototyping, from paper to digital.
- **Build (software track).** Build strategies, custom code versus no-code and low-code; shipping a minimal working product; using AI-assisted development tools responsibly; scoping technical debt for an MVP.
- **Build (hardware track).** From CAD to functional prototype; using the makerspace and rapid fabrication; bill of materials and early cost thinking.
- **Validate.** Build, measure, learn; designing a usability test; running a pilot; early product metrics such as activation and retention; deciding to continue, change, or stop.
- **Launch.** Positioning and messaging; choosing early channels; pricing basics; planning a launch; preparing for MakerLaunch or the next stage.

### Appendix B. Competency rubric sketch

A starting point for assessing each competency, to be developed before launch.

| Competency | Entry | Proficient |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Discover | Can describe a problem they care about | Has validated the problem through at least twenty conversations |
| Define | Can state who the user is | Has a clear value proposition and a scoped MVP |
| Design | Can sketch a solution | Has a testable prototype users can react to |
| Build | Can describe what to build | Has a working MVP or functional prototype |
| Validate | Can name what to test | Has run a test and made a continue, change, or stop decision |
| Launch | Can name a first channel | Has a launch plan and early traction signals |

### Appendix C. Current tools

This list changes quickly and should be reviewed before each year. The names are examples, not requirements.

- **Design and prototyping:** Figma under education licensing.
- **No-code and low-code build:** current web and app builders such as Bubble, Webflow, Glide, and Airtable.
- **AI-assisted development:** current AI coding and app-generation tools such as Replit, Cursor, v0, and Lovable.
- **Hardware prototyping:** the makerspace, CAD tools, and rapid fabrication equipment.
